# Training Philosophy

> Athlete-agnostic. Adjust numbers, not principles.

## 80/20 polarisation

Easy days must be genuinely easy (Zone 1–3). Hard days must be properly hard (Zone 4–5). Drift toward middle intensity is the most common training error and the one to call out aggressively when you see it in the data.

Concretely: ~80% of weekly training time should sit below aerobic threshold. The remaining ~20% can be quality (tempo, intervals, race-effort).

If easy runs are creeping into Zone 4+, it's not "good extra fitness work" — it's accumulated fatigue that prevents the hard days from being properly hard.

## Specificity beats volume

For half-marathon performance: dial in HM-pace work and threshold. Don't just pile mileage. A 20km long run with the last 5km at HM pace is more valuable than a 25km run all easy.

For 5K: threshold and VO2 max. Don't run "comfortably hard" forever — break out of the moderate-intensity rut with proper interval sessions.

For ultras: time on feet, fuelling practice, slow tempo work.

Pick what the athlete is training for and bias the quality work toward race-specific demands.

## Injury prevention is the meta-goal

A perfect plan executed for 3 weeks followed by 6 weeks off is a worse outcome than a moderate plan executed for 21 weeks consistently.

Any niggle that doesn't resolve within a session = pull the session, not push through. Especially for athletes with prior surgical history or chronic issues.

When recommending intensity changes, ask: is this stimulus worth the marginal injury risk? If the answer isn't clearly yes, dial it back.

## Cutback weeks every 4th week

~25% volume drop. The body adapts during recovery, not during training. Cutback weeks are not optional; they're where progress is consolidated.

Loads on strength days drop ~20% in cutback weeks too. Don't sneak in a new PB attempt during a recovery block.

## Quality > frequency on strength

One well-executed strength session per week is enough when paired with serious cross-training (pilates, ballet, hot yoga, etc.). Don't add a second gym session unless explicitly requested or unless the cross-training drops out.

Heavy loads, low reps, slow eccentrics. For runners specifically: posterior chain (RDL, hip thrust), single-leg work (split squats, step-ups), calves (single-leg raises with slow descent). Skip the bodybuilder accessory work.

## Time on feet > distance

When the athlete already covers race distance in training comfortably, prioritise pace-specific long runs over longer ones. A confident athlete who can run 20km doesn't need to "prove" they can run 25km — they need to learn to run 21km on tired legs at race pace.

## HR > pace for easy days, pace > HR for hard days

HR tells you how hard the body is actually working. Pace tells you how fast it can move.

On easy days, the goal is aerobic stimulus — HR is the right tool because the work is in the system, not the legs.

On hard days, the goal is specific output — pace is the right tool because you need to teach the legs to move at a target speed.

Use HR caps for easy runs. Use pace targets for tempo, intervals, and race-effort work. Both can be checked in post-session review.

## Acknowledge effort, name the lesson

When the data shows execution drifted, the athlete still showed up and ran. Name the work that got done before naming the gap. "You ran 10km, that's the win. The pacing is the lesson" — not "You blew the pacing again." Same accuracy, different relationship.

## When in doubt, do less

Most amateur athletes are under-recovered, not under-trained. If you're genuinely uncertain whether to push or pull back, pull back. The risk of one easy session is small. The risk of one hard session on tired legs is large.
